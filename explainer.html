Starting Point

A brain that already sees

Meta built a model called DINOv2 — a neural network trained on millions of images. It already understands visual concepts like shapes, edges, and textures. It just doesn't know anything about fonts specifically.

Instead of building from scratch, we take this existing brain and teach it about fonts. This process is called fine-tuning — like sending it to font school.

Think of hiring a professional artist who already understands shapes and composition, then teaching them to identify typography — instead of teaching a toddler to draw first.

Efficient Learning

Changing just 0.4% of the brain

DINOv2 has 86 million parameters (think of these as the "neurons" or knobs in its brain). Retraining all of them would be slow, expensive, and could make the model forget what it already knows.

Instead, we use a technique called LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation). It freezes 99.6% of the brain and only adds a thin layer of new, trainable connections.

Core Idea

Turning fonts into fingerprints

The model takes any image and converts it into 256 numbers — a fingerprint that captures the font's visual essence. Each number represents something about the font's style (we don't know exactly what each number means, but together they encode things like weight, serif presence, roundness, etc).

The key insight: similar-looking fonts should have similar fingerprints.

How It Learns

Learning from comparison

The training process is called contrastive learning. The core idea is simple: show the model pairs of fonts, tell it which are the "same" and which are "different", and it gradually learns what makes fonts look alike.

What a training batch looks like

Each batch contains 64 font images: we pick 32 font families, and grab 2 images from each. Here's a simplified view of one batch:

One training batch — 32 families × 2 samples each = 64 images

Sample 2

...and 22 more families in each batch

For each batch, the model is told: the two samples in the same row (same color) are the "same" — pull their fingerprints closer. Every sample in a different row is "different" — push their fingerprints apart.

After seeing thousands of these batches, the model learns the visual DNA of each font family.

Like showing someone flashcards of dogs. "Golden retriever puppy and adult golden retriever — same breed. Golden retriever and poodle — different breed." After thousands of flashcards, they can recognize any golden retriever, even one they've never seen.

Training Signals

Three teachers, one student

The model gets feedback from three different "teachers" at the same time. Each teacher focuses on a different aspect of what makes fonts similar:

Family Identity
Weight: 1.0
The main teacher. "Roboto Bold and Roboto Light are the same family — make their fingerprints close. Roboto and Playfair are different families — push them apart."
Tag Similarity
Weight: 0.3
A softer teacher. Fonts tagged "geometric" or "rounded" on Google Fonts should be somewhat near each other, even if they're different families. Teaches cross-family visual similarity.
Tag Prediction
Weight: 0.2
A side teacher. "Given this font image, predict its tags: is it serif? bold? decorative?" Forces the model to learn recognizable visual properties.
Real-World Robustness

Preparing for messy cameras

We train on clean, digitally rendered text. But users send messy camera photos — with weird lighting, angles, blur, and backgrounds. To bridge this gap, we deliberately mess up the training images with random effects:

Like training a sommelier by sometimes giving them wine in a plastic cup at a loud party — they still need to identify the wine. No excuses.

Measuring Success

Is the right font in the results?

We need a way to measure if the model is actually working. Here's how we test it:

1. Take a font we know (e.g. Roboto)
2. Render some text in that font
3. Mess it up with camera-like effects (blur, tilt, noise)
4. Ask the model: "find the closest fonts in the database"
5. Check: is the correct font family somewhere in the results?
